Biography

I am a development economist specializing in the measurement of food and water insecurity in low-income countries. My work bridges rigorous academic research with practical application, helping nonprofits and development organizations make evidence-based decisions that improve lives.

Over my career, I have partnered with nearly 100 nonprofit organizations across more than 25 countries, conducting research and evaluations that have influenced program design and policy. My approach combines quantitative research methods with extensive field experience, ensuring that measurement tools and evaluation frameworks are both scientifically sound and practically useful.

I earned my PhD in Development Economics from [University Name], where my dissertation focused on developing and validating measurement scales for household food and water insecurity. Since then, I have published extensively on measurement methodology, impact evaluation, and the effectiveness of development interventions.

My research has been supported by organizations including the World Bank, USAID, Gates Foundation, and numerous bilateral donors. I serve on the editorial boards of several development journals and regularly review proposals for major funding agencies.

Beyond research, I am committed to building capacity within the development sector. I have trained hundreds of practitioners in evidence-based program design, data collection methods, and impact evaluation techniques. I believe that rigorous research should be accessible and actionable for the organizations working on the front lines of global development.
